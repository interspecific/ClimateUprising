Land Use Transformation Plan to Mitigate Climate Change

Why Land Matters

Natural climate solutions can deliver up to one-third of the carbon mitigation needed by 2030 to keep global warming below 2°C, alongside rapid fossil fuel phaseout. In the U.S., this could mean mitigating ~1.2 billion metric tons CO₂e annually.

Core Strategies

  • Use peer-reviewed science: Apply studies like Griscom et al. and IPCC AR6 to guide carbon targets.
  • Establish precise baselines: Map land cover, carbon stocks, and social vulnerability using authoritative datasets.
  • Map and prioritize zones: Overlay carbon, ecosystem services, and equity metrics to guide action.
  • Set acreage and mitigation targets: Define time-bound, science-aligned goals for restoration and sequestration.
  • Integrate ecosystem service modeling: Quantify co-benefits like flood mitigation and biodiversity gains.
  • Develop robust monitoring: Combine satellite, LiDAR, field data, and citizen science for accountability.
  • Align with global pathways: Coordinate nature-based solutions with deep decarbonization.

Why This Matters

Natural climate solutions can deliver up to one-third of global mitigation needed by 2030. In the U.S., up to 1.2 billion metric tons of CO₂-equivalent can be mitigated annually. A science-based approach ensures these actions are effective, equitable, and resilient—building public trust and safeguarding people and nature.

Policy Actions

  • Federal reforestation and agroforestry programs
  • Soil carbon and regenerative farming incentives
  • Grazing land transformation strategies
  • Wetland and peatland restoration funds
  • Grassland and prairie protection
  • Urban canopy initiatives targeting heat-vulnerable areas

The Polluters Pay Climate Superfund Act: A Game-Changer for Climate Justice

The proposed Polluters Pay Climate Superfund Act of 2025 in California is a groundbreaking step toward climate accountability. It would require fossil fuel companies to pay for the damage their emissions have caused over decades—covering climate harms from 1990 to 2024—and channel those funds into a dedicated climate superfund to support adaptation, recovery, and resilience across the state.

This model echoes the federal EPA Superfund program, which since 1980 has forced polluting industries to pay for the cleanup of toxic waste sites, rather than leaving taxpayers with the bill. Just as the EPA Superfund made polluters responsible for local environmental disasters, the Climate Superfund would make the biggest carbon polluters responsible for the global climate crisis they helped create.

The Climate Superfund is not just smart policy—it’s fair policy. It ensures that fossil fuel giants who profited from decades of emissions help cover the cost of extreme weather, sea-level rise, wildfires, and other climate damages, rather than pushing that burden onto everyday Californians and future generations.

This is a model we should take national: hold polluters accountable, fund climate solutions, and build a just and resilient future.
